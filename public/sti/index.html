This looks at two questions for each infection: if I sleep with a random person, how likely are they to have it? (prevalence), and if they do and we don't use protection, how likely is it to pass to me? (transmission). Then the part that gets buried under fear and shame: how bad is it, really, given modern treatment?

The numbers come from CDC surveillance, NHANES, WHO, and peer-reviewed meta-analyses; every figure below was gathered and then independently fact-checked against primary sources. Prevalence is US general-adult point prevalence. Transmission is shown per single act where the literature supports it, and per partnership where it doesn't — the two are labeled and not interchangeable. Where the data genuinely don't exist, it says so rather than inventing a number.

What "severity" means here

The severity number is a 1–10 judgment of how much harm the infection actually does to you, all things considered, assuming modern US treatment access. On its own the digit is meaningless, so here is what each level means in concrete terms, and which infections land there.

Two things this bakes in. The score assumes the infection is found and treated — untreated, several of these climb sharply. And hepatitis B and C are genuinely bimodal (trivial for most, serious for the chronic minority), so their single number is a blend.

The single most clarifying view. Left-to-right: how likely a random adult partner is to carry it. Bottom-to-top: how much harm it does if you get it, all things considered, with modern US care.

Actual risk per unprotected act

Prevalence × per-act transmission = your real chance of acquiring it from one unprotected act with a random partner (most of whom don't have it). Only the five infections with defensible per-act data appear here.

If you're a man who has sex with men

The general-population numbers understate risk substantially for MSM, for two compounding reasons: prevalence is higher — sometimes far higher — and receptive anal intercourse is the single highest-efficiency sexual route for HIV, roughly 12–18× the per-act risk of receptive vaginal sex. The two effects multiply.

Also elevated in MSM but not plotted: hepatitis C (negligible heterosexually, but meaningfully transmitted among HIV-positive MSM through rectal exposure) and anal HPV (near-universal in HIV-positive MSM, driving anal-cancer risk that is uncommon in the general population). The severity picture is otherwise the same as above — what changes for MSM is the likelihood of acquiring, not how bad each infection is once you have it.

How bad is each, really

HIV — Feared as a death sentence. Treated, lifespan is near-normal and it becomes untransmittable. Without treatment, fatal immune collapse; on ART, a managed chronic illness.

Gonorrhea — Still curable in one visit — the genuine worry is escalating antibiotic resistance, not the infection itself. PID/infertility if untreated; drug-resistant strains are emerging.

Chlamydia — Common and fully curable; the danger is going undetected — a burden that falls mostly on women. Usually silent; untreated it can cause PID and infertility in women.

HPV — Ubiquitous and usually self-clearing; the real (cancer) risk is confined to a few strains the vaccine covers. A few strains cause cervical/throat/anal cancer — rare, and screen/vaccine-preventable.

Genital herpes (HSV-2) — Among the most stigmatized STIs, yet medically minor for nearly everyone who has it. Recurrent genital sores; for most, infrequent and mild.

Oral herpes (HSV-1) — So common it's nearly universal by old age — effectively a background fact of being human. Cold sores; occasionally causes genital herpes.

Syphilis — Trivial to cure early, dangerous only if missed — and US cases are rising sharply, so it's back on the table. Untreated → neurological/cardiac tertiary disease; congenital syphilis in newborns.

Trichomoniasis — The most common curable STI worldwide, and among the most medically trivial. Often asymptomatic; discharge and irritation when symptomatic.

Hepatitis B — Vaccine-preventable and cleared by most adults — the chronic minority carries essentially all the harm. The 5–10% of adults who stay chronic face cirrhosis / liver cancer.

Hepatitis C — Barely a sexually transmitted infection heterosexually — and now curable in a couple of months. Chronic infection → cirrhosis / liver cancer if left untreated for years.

M. genitalium — Only recently recognized; usually mild, but increasingly hard to treat as resistance spreads. Urethritis / cervicitis; macrolide resistance is now common.

Caveats worth keeping in mind

Prevalence is general-population. Your real partner pool may differ a lot from the US average — by age, region, orientation, and network. MSM, in particular, face substantially higher per-act and prevalence figures for HIV, syphilis, hepatitis C and anal HPV than the general-adult numbers shown here.

Per-act vs. per-partnership. For six of these, per-act transmission has never been cleanly measured; the honest best-available figure is the chance of transmission over an ongoing partnership, which is higher than any single act. Don't compare the two columns directly.

Severity assumes access to care. Almost every "how bad is it" score collapses toward trivial if diagnosed and treated. The real harm in this whole table lives in the gap between infection and treatment — asymptomatic infections that go undetected, and people without access. That gap falls disproportionately on women (PID, infertility, cervical cancer, neonatal transmission).

Directionality matters. Receptive partners generally face higher per-act risk than insertive partners. The headline per-act figures here use the female→male (insertive-male) direction where a choice was needed.
